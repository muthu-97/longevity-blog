<p class="lede">Your DNA is under attack right now. Not occasionally — constantly. Around 10,000 to 100,000 times today, inside each of your 37 trillion cells, something will nick, break, or corrupt a DNA strand. The only reason that doesn't kill you is a repair crew working around the clock. Hallmark #1 is what happens when the crew starts falling behind.</p>

In the 1980s, Valter Longo was studying yeast cells. He found that when he starved them, they lived three times longer than normal. Then he broke one specific DNA repair gene before starving them — and the starvation did nothing. They died at the normal pace regardless.

His conclusion: **longevity isn't just about how little damage you accumulate. It's about how aggressively your body repairs the damage it constantly gets.** The repair system is the aging system.

The number: your DNA is damaged **10,000 to 100,000 times per day. Per cell.** Not per body — per single cell. Multiplied across 37 trillion cells, that's an incomprehensible volume of damage being caught and fixed, every single day you're alive. When the fixing slows down, the errors pile up. That's Hallmark #1.

**Free radicals** are unstable oxygen molecules produced as a byproduct of energy production in your mitochondria. Every breath creates some. They punch holes in DNA strands.

**Replication errors** happen every time a cell divides. The copy machine makes roughly one mistake per billion letters. That sounds tiny — but across trillions of cell divisions over a lifetime, those errors accumulate.

**UV radiation** slices DNA directly. Every sunburn is an episode of mass DNA damage in your skin cells. Your body repairs most of it. Miss one repair in the wrong gene, and you have melanoma.

**Chemicals:** alcohol is directly genotoxic (damages DNA). So are nitrosamines in processed meat, benzene in cigarette smoke, and a long list of industrial compounds.

**Chronic inflammation** sends reactive chemicals from immune cells that damage nearby DNA. This is why the inflammation hallmark (#12) accelerates all the others — it keeps generating new DNA damage upstream.

## Your body's repair systems

You have four major repair systems, each targeting a different type of damage:

| Repair system | What it fixes | The plain version |
|---|---|---|
| Base excision repair | Single damaged letters | Spell-checker fixing typos |
| Nucleotide excision repair | Bulky damage, UV lesions | Cutting out a corrupted paragraph and retyping it |
| Mismatch repair | Errors made during copying | Proofreader comparing the copy to the original |
| Double-strand break repair | Both strands of DNA completely snapped | Emergency welding a severed cable |

When you're 25, this crew is fast, well-resourced, and catches almost everything. By 60, the crew is slower. Errors that would have been caught are now getting through. The factory starts printing with uncorrected typos — and those typos compound with every subsequent copy.

## How a typo becomes disease

Most errors land in parts of the DNA that don't code for anything critical — silent. But when damage hits the wrong gene:

- A **tumour suppressor gene** (like p53, sometimes called "the guardian of the genome") gets disrupted → the brakes on cell division fail → uncontrolled growth → cancer
- An **oncogene** (a gene that signals cells to grow) gets stuck in "on" → same result

Beyond cancer, accumulated DNA damage:
- Triggers **cellular senescence** — the cell stops dividing permanently and starts leaking inflammatory signals into surrounding tissue (Hallmark #6)
- Depletes **stem cell populations** — fewer spare cells to replace damaged ones (Hallmark #9)
- Scrambles **gene expression patterns** — bleeding into Hallmark #3 (Epigenetic Alterations)

<p class="takeaway">Your DNA gets damaged 10,000 to 100,000 times a day. Aging is what happens when the <span>repair crew gets tired and the errors start compounding</span>. Your job — and your members' job — is to keep that crew well-funded.</p>

## How we see it at Club EverStrong

<div class="ce">
  <div class="tag">From the studio floor</div>
  <p>You won't have a member walk in and say "I think I have genomic instability." But the profile is recognisable: decades of smoking or heavy drinking, chronic poor sleep, high hs-CRP on their blood panel, slow recovery from training sessions, frequent illness. That's a person whose repair crew is underfunded and losing ground.</p>
  <p>The intervention order matters here. You fix the biggest damage sources first — sleep, smoking, alcohol — before optimising anything else. Sleep is when the majority of DNA repair happens. A person who trains hard but sleeps five hours is like running a factory on full production while turning off the maintenance crew at night. The output looks fine for a while. Then things start breaking faster than they can be fixed.</p>
  <p>On the blood panel: hs-CRP above 1.0 mg/L and elevated ferritin (above 200 in women, 300 in men) are practical proxies. They tell you the environment inside the body is generating ongoing oxidative stress — which means more DNA damage happening around the clock, and less repair bandwidth to catch it.</p>
</div>

**Prevention — what the evidence actually supports:**

| Intervention | Mechanism | Evidence grade |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep 7–9 hrs | Main repair window — most DNA repair happens at night | Solid |
| Zone 2 cardio | Boosts NAD+, the fuel that powers repair enzymes | Solid |
| Resistance training | Activates DNA damage response pathways | Moderate–solid |
| Fasting / caloric restriction | Reduces free radical load, activates repair genes | Solid in animals, promising in humans |
| No smoking | Removes one of the most potent DNA mutagens | Very solid |
| Sun protection | Prevents UV-induced strand breaks | Very solid |
| Reducing alcohol | Alcohol is directly genotoxic | Solid |
| Cruciferous vegetables | Activate NRF2, which upregulates repair enzymes | Moderate |

**On drugs:** NAD+ precursors (NMN and NR) aim to restore the fuel that powers the repair enzyme PARP1, which drops ~50% by age 50. Human studies show NAD+ levels do go up with supplementation. Whether that translates meaningfully to better repair in practice is still being established — promising, not proven, with an estimated 10–20% improvement in repair capacity markers. The TAME trial on metformin (which reduces mitochondrial free radical production) is the largest longevity drug trial ever attempted and results are pending. These are optimisations. They sit on top of a solid lifestyle base, not in place of one.

## Clinical measurement

You can't directly count DNA damage in a standard clinic. But proxies tell you a lot:

| Marker | What it signals | Availability |
|---|---|---|
| hs-CRP | Chronic inflammation = ongoing DNA damage environment | Standard blood panel |
| Ferritin (elevated) | Excess iron generates free radicals → DNA damage | Standard blood panel |
| 8-OHdG (urine) | Direct oxidative DNA damage byproduct | Specialised labs |
| Biological age clocks (GrimAge, DunedinPACE) | Epigenetic read of cumulative damage history | TruDiagnostic, Elysium |
| Telomere length | Proxy for cumulative replication and repair history | LifeLength, TruDiagnostic |

## The proper names

<dl class="terms">
  <dt>Genomic instability</dt>
  <dd>The accumulation of DNA damage over time, combined with a declining ability to repair it. The first and most upstream of the twelve hallmarks. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#genomic-instability">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>Free radicals (reactive oxygen species)</dt>
  <dd>Unstable molecules with an unpaired electron that damage DNA, proteins, and cell membranes. Produced as a normal byproduct of metabolism. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#free-radicals">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>Tumour suppressor gene</dt>
  <dd>A gene that slows or stops cell division and triggers repair or cell death when damage is detected. p53 is the most studied. Mutations here remove the brakes on uncontrolled growth. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#tumour-suppressor">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>p53</dt>
  <dd>The most important tumour suppressor protein — detects DNA damage, pauses cell division, and triggers repair or programmed cell death. Mutated in over 50% of human cancers. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#p53">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide)</dt>
  <dd>A molecule found in every cell, essential for energy production and DNA repair. Declines ~50% between age 20 and 50. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#nad">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>PARP1</dt>
  <dd>The primary enzyme that detects and repairs DNA single-strand breaks. Consumes NAD+ as fuel. When NAD+ is depleted, PARP1 slows and errors accumulate. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#parp1">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>8-OHdG</dt>
  <dd>8-hydroxy-2'-deoxyguanosine — a damaged DNA base released into urine when oxidative DNA damage is repaired. Used as a biomarker for oxidative stress. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#8-ohdg">definition →</a></span></dd>
</dl>
