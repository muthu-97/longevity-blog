<p class="lede">Every cell in your body — all 37 trillion of them — carries the exact same instruction manual. Two metres of it, coiled down to fit inside a space 1/100th the width of a human hair. That manual is your DNA. Once you see how it works, aging stops being mysterious. It becomes mechanical.</p>

In 2003, a boy named Sam Berns was born in the US. Normal delivery, healthy baby. By age 2 he had stopped growing. By age 10 he was 100 cm tall, weighed 15 kg, had lost all his hair, and his heart was aging at seven times the normal rate. He died at 17 from a heart attack.

Sam had Progeria — a condition affecting roughly 1 in 4 million children. Fewer than 400 kids live with it at any given moment worldwide. They look like 70-year-olds by the time they're 10.

The cause: **a single wrong letter in his DNA.** Not a paragraph. Not a chapter. One letter. Out of 3.2 billion.

That one wrong letter produced a broken protein. That broken protein caused the walls of his cell's control room to collapse. His cells couldn't divide, couldn't repair themselves, and died early. His body aged at seven times the normal speed.

That's how much power one letter in your DNA holds.

**The cell** is the factory. It takes in fuel, makes products, repairs itself, and either divides to make a copy or eventually dies. Every hallmark of aging is one part of this factory breaking down.

**The nucleus** is the vault. It holds all the instructions and nothing gets built in the factory without orders from here. Sam Berns' nucleus walls were collapsing — when the vault fails, the DNA inside gets damaged constantly.

**Chromosomes** are how you fit 2 metres of DNA into a vault 0.006mm wide. The DNA coils tightly around proteins called histones, like thread on a spool, into 46 chromosomes. How tightly those spools are wound actually controls which recipes get read. That winding pattern is what Hallmark #3 (Epigenetic Alterations) disrupts as you age.

**Genes** are individual recipes. Each one says: make this specific protein. You have about 20,000. Sam Berns had a mutation in the LMNA gene — one letter wrong — which produced a broken version of the protein that holds the nucleus wall together.

**Telomeres** are the protective caps at the end of each chromosome — like the plastic tip on a shoelace that stops it from fraying. Every time a cell divides and copies its DNA, the copy machine can't quite reach the very end. So each copy loses a tiny slice of the telomere. After 50–70 divisions, the cap is gone. The cell panics, stops dividing, and either sits there leaking inflammatory signals or dies. That's Hallmark #2: Telomere Attrition.

The nucleus never opens. Instead it makes a photocopy of the recipe (called mRNA) small enough to slip out and reach the assembler (the ribosome). The ribosome reads the photocopy and strings amino acids together into a protein. The protein then goes off and does its job.

Break any link in this chain and the function downstream fails:
- DNA damaged → broken recipe → wrong protein made
- Gene silenced by epigenetic change → recipe never photocopied → protein never made
- Protein misfolds after assembly → faulty worker clogs the factory

Most of the 12 hallmarks break something in this chain.

## Proteins aren't just for your muscles

Here's the thing gym culture got half right. When you eat protein — chicken, eggs, dal, paneer — your gut breaks those chains apart into individual amino acids. Think of it as breaking a necklace into separate beads. Those beads enter your bloodstream and form a shared **amino acid pool** that every cell draws from simultaneously.

The pool doesn't earmark beads for muscle. It supplies:

- Enzymes that drive every chemical reaction in your body
- Hormones (insulin, cortisol, thyroid)
- Structural scaffolding (collagen in skin, bone, tendons)
- Immune defenders (antibodies)
- Transport proteins (haemoglobin, which carries oxygen)
- DNA repair crews
- Neurotransmitter building blocks (serotonin, dopamine)

And yes — muscle fibres (actin and myosin), which happen to be the single biggest draw because muscle makes up about 40% of your body weight.

The fitness industry simplified it to "protein equals muscle" because muscle is visible. The rest — better enzyme function, stronger immunity, more efficient DNA repair — doesn't show in a photo. But it's all coming from the same pool.

<p class="takeaway">Your DNA is a 2-metre instruction manual packed into a 0.006mm vault, organised into 46 chapters, with 20,000 recipes. The recipes build proteins. Proteins are <span>literally everything your body does</span>. Aging is what happens when the manual gets damaged and the copies get sloppier.</p>

## The proper names

<dl class="terms">
  <dt>Cell</dt>
  <dd>The basic unit of life. Each human cell is self-contained: it generates energy, makes proteins, copies itself, and eventually dies. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#cell">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>Nucleus</dt>
  <dd>The membrane-enclosed compartment inside most cells that houses the DNA. Separated from the rest of the cell by a double membrane called the nuclear envelope. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#nucleus">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>Chromosome</dt>
  <dd>A tightly coiled length of DNA wrapped around histone proteins. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) in every cell nucleus. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#chromosome">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>Gene</dt>
  <dd>A specific sequence of DNA that encodes the instructions to build one protein. ~20,000 protein-coding genes in the human genome. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#gene">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>Telomere</dt>
  <dd>Repeated DNA sequences (TTAGGG) capping the ends of chromosomes. Protect against DNA loss during replication. Shorten with each cell division. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#telomere">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>mRNA (messenger RNA)</dt>
  <dd>A single-stranded photocopy of a gene that carries the recipe out of the nucleus to the ribosome for assembly. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#mrna">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>Ribosome</dt>
  <dd>The molecular machine that reads mRNA and strings amino acids together to build a protein. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#ribosome">definition →</a></span></dd>
  <dt>Amino acids</dt>
  <dd>The 20 building blocks of proteins. Obtained from dietary protein after digestion. Form the shared amino acid pool that supplies every protein-building process in the body. <span class="more"><a href="/glossary/#amino-acids">definition →</a></span></dd>
</dl>
